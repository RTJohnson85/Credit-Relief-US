Bankruptcy

Millions of Americans struggle with debt every single day. They fight just to make the minimum payments due and keep creditors from hounding them. For many people, it feels like their lives are out of control and they see no path forward to find relief. Debt consolidation is a viable choice, but may not be for everyone. Another option is bankruptcy. Is this the right path for you?

What Is Bankruptcy?

Bankruptcy is a legal solution designed to help US citizens struggling with overwhelming debt. Depending on the type of bankruptcy you file, you may have a portion of your debt erased or even all of the discharged. However, it’s only a viable option for certain types of debt.

You cannot use bankruptcy to clear debt stemming from federally-backed student loans, most types of personal income tax debt, fines related to criminal charges, or any child support obligations.

For those who cannot see a light at the end of the debt tunnel, bankruptcy might be the right solution. However, don’t assume that this option allows you to wipe away consumer debt without any repercussions. Completing your bankruptcy will mean dealing with significant restrictions, and in some cases, you’ll have to sacrifice assets to pay your creditors. Finally, a bankruptcy stays on your credit report for a very long time.

What to Know About Bankruptcy

According to the federal government, almost 550,000 Americans filed for bankruptcy in 2020 (the most recent year for which statistics are available). That’s down from almost 780,000 in 2019, but it is still a stark figure that depicts just how many people in the nation are facing financial crises.

With that being said, bankruptcy should only be used as a last resort. You should exhaust all your other options, including debt consolidation, debt validation, credit counseling, debt settlement, and any others before filing bankrupt.

  • Bankruptcy can stay on your credit report for up to 10 years
  • You will likely lose most or all of your credit cards
  • Most lenders will be unwilling to offer you a loan or new credit card afterward
  • Bankruptcy can affect your employment prospects
  • There is no guarantee that your debts will be erased
  • You may be required to sell off some of your assets to pay your creditors

The Two Most Popular Types of Bankruptcy

While there are six types of bankruptcy in the United States, the two most popular for consumers are Chapter 7 and Chapter 13. Both offer an end to your debt woes, but they are drastically different.

Chapter 7

The most common type of bankruptcy in the United States is Chapter 7. This is a “liquidation-style” process and is probably what you think of when you hear the word bankruptcy. In this situation, you’ll need to pass a means test to make sure you qualify (high-income/high-asset consumers are excluded from this option). Next, a court-appointed trustee will appraise all non-exempt assets and sell them off. The money generated will be used to pay your creditors. Expect this process to take around six months. Your (qualifying) debts will be cleared afterward.

Chapter 13

Chapter 13 is a different process entirely. This is more like debt restructuring, although some types of debt can be eliminated. If you do not qualify for Chapter 7 and you earn a regular paycheck (or have another stable stream of income), this process may be the better choice. You are given the ability to repay your creditors within three to five years after your debts have been restructured.

Both types of bankruptcy offer protection from your creditors through what’s called an automatic stay. Merely beginning the process of filing is all that’s required to stop your creditors from communicating with you. This will also stop lawsuits, foreclosures, evictions, and other actions taken by your creditors.
